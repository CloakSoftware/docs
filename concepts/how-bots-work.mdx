---
title: "How bots work"
description: "A Cloak bot is a full end-to-end encrypted member of every server it joins. Here is what that means for you."
---

A Cloak bot is a program you run. It connects to Cloak's hosted service, joins servers as a member, and sends and receives encrypted messages. What sets it apart from a bot on most chat platforms is where the encryption happens.

## Your bot is an encrypted member

On many platforms, a bot is a privileged client of a server that can read message content in the clear. On Cloak, your bot is a first-class encrypted member, the same as any person in the server.

That means:

- Your bot holds its own cryptographic identity.
- Your bot receives each server's encryption key through Cloak's normal key exchange.
- All encryption and decryption happen inside your process.

The Cloak service relays encrypted bytes between members. It never sees plaintext message content, and it never sees an unwrapped key.

<Note>
  Because your bot does the crypto itself, it needs to hold real keys. Persisting those keys safely is the single most important operational detail. See [Keys and the keystore](/concepts/keys-and-keystore).
</Note>

## The lifecycle of a message

When someone sends a message your bot can see:

<Steps>
  <Step title="A member sends an encrypted message">
    Their app encrypts the content with the server's key and sends the ciphertext to Cloak.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cloak relays it to your bot">
    The service delivers the ciphertext to your bot's session as part of the [firehose](/concepts/message-delivery).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Your bot decrypts it locally">
    The SDK decrypts the content with the key your bot holds and hands you a `Message` with readable `content`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Your bot replies">
    When you call `msg.reply()`, the SDK encrypts your text in the process and sends the ciphertext back through Cloak.
  </Step>
</Steps>

At no point does the plaintext leave your machine unencrypted.

## What you are responsible for

Because your bot is a real member holding real keys, a few responsibilities sit with you rather than with the platform.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Guard the token" icon="key">
    The bot token grants full encrypted membership. Keep it in an environment variable or secret manager, never in code.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Keep the keystore" icon="database">
    The keystore file holds your bot's identity and its live keys. Persist it and protect it. See [Keys and the keystore](/concepts/keys-and-keystore).
  </Card>
  <Card title="Host the process" icon="server">
    Your bot is a program you run and keep running. Cloak does not host it for you.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Handle permissions" icon="shield">
    Declare what your bot needs, and check what each server granted before acting. See [Permissions](/concepts/permissions).
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## The API is familiar on purpose

The SDK is shaped like [discord.js](https://discord.js.org). You get a `Client`, you attach listeners for events like `messageCreate` and `guildCreate`, and messages carry a `reply()` method. If you have built a Discord bot, porting it is mostly mechanical.

Keep one thing in mind: the resemblance is in the shape of the code, not the wire protocol. A Cloak bot talks to Cloak. It cannot connect to Discord, and a Discord bot cannot connect to Cloak.
